Understanding Crosswords

  • word puzzle
  • square or a rectangular grid
  • white- and black-shaded squares
  • fill the white squares with letters (forming words or phrases) by solving clues
  • left to right (across) and top to bottom (down)
  • every letter is “checked”
  • each answer must contain at least three letters
  • in US, shaded squares are typically limited to about 1/6th
  • in Commonwealth Nations, lattice-like structure with a higher percentage of shaded squares (around 25%), leaving about half the letters in an answer unchecked
  • grid should have 180-degree rotational symmetry (radial)
  • pattern appears the same if the crossword is turned upside down
  • all white cells be orthogonally contiguous
  • Variants:
    • barred crosswords use bold lines between squares (instead of shaded squares) to separate answers
    • circular designs with answers entered either radially or in concentric circles
  • "free form" crosswords or "criss-cross" puzzles have simple asymmetric designs
  • standard size: 15×15 squares
  • special sizes: 21×21, 23×23, 25×25
  • clues appear outside the grid: an across list and a down list
  • first cell of each entry contains a referenced number
  • numbers are never repeated
  • numbered cells are numbered consecutively, (left to right across each row, from top row and proceeding downward)
  • capitalisation of answer letters is conventionally ignored
  • Japanese crossword grids:
    • shaded cells may not share a side
    • corner squares must be white
  • Swedish-style grid (picture crosswords):
    • no clue numbers
    • clues are contained in the cells which do not contain answers
    • arrows indicate in which direction the clues have to be answered: vertical or horizontal
    • grid often has one or more photos replacing a block of squares as a clue to one or several answers
    • no symmetry in the grid but instead often have a common theme
  • for NYT crosswords, difficulty increases throughout the week: Monday puzzles are the easiest and the puzzles get harder each day until Saturday. The Sunday puzzle is about the same level of difficulty as a weekday-size Thursday puzzle

Understanding Current 3D Crosswords

  • x (across), y (down), z (away, to) where x, y, z are odd numbers
  • x, y, z may or may not be equal
  • all words run from one side to the other (except when direction changed)
  • words longer than x, y, or z can change direction
  • notations:
    • for across, 1ac-5
    • for down, 1d-5
    • for away, 1aw-5
    • for towards, 1to-5 (rarely used)
    • for direction changes, 2aw,8ac-3

Analysing Current 3D Crosswords

What it succeeds to do?

  • provide a base for 3D crosswords with words running in all three directions
  • odd number of squares in each direction
  • every letter is “checked”
  • easy visualisation on paper
  • rotational symmetry

What it fails to do?

  • always follows the same lattice structure
  • fails to follow regular crossword structures
  • high shaded-square percentage
  • white cells are not orthogonally contiguous
  • though x, y, z can be expanded as needed, they are rarely raised above 5
  • order of words does not conform to regular crosswords

What can be worked upon?

  • decreased shaded-squares
  • increase white cells & words
  • white cells be orthogonally contiguous
  • create more lattice structures
  • rotational symmetry across the diagonal
  • bigger lattice sizes
  • order of words
  • use of longer words without direction changes
  • visual representation

What can be removed?

  • direction changes
  • complicated notations that come with direction changes
